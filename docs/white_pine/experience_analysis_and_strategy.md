## 2. Interview Strategy & Tactics

Your goal is to re-frame the narrative from "Does he know our specific tools?" to "Can he solve our underlying problems better than anyone else?"

### A. How to Frame Your Strengths

*   **You are a "Builder," not just an "Admin":** When they ask about experience with a tool you don't know, pivot to your deeper knowledge.
    > *"While I haven't used MarketSharp specifically, my core strength is in understanding how data-driven systems work from the ground up. I've built entire applications that manage complex business logic. Learning a specific CRM's interface is the easy part for me; my value is that I can diagnose problems at a deeper level and identify opportunities for optimization that a typical admin might miss."*

*   **Connect DevOps to Process Improvement:** Your DevOps and automation skills are your secret weapon.
    > *"I'm passionate about making things efficient. In my own development work, I got tired of manually setting up environments, so I wrote a script to automate the entire process. I would bring that same mindset here. My goal would be to not just troubleshoot issues, but to find their root cause and see if we can improve the workflow or system to prevent them from happening again."*

*   **Use PHP to Your Advantage with WordPress:**
    > *"My experience with WordPress is from the code-level up. Because I'm very comfortable with PHP, I'm not limited to what the admin panel allows. If there's a need for a custom tweak, a performance optimization, or a bug fix within a theme or plugin, I have the skills to go directly into the code and solve it."*

### B. How to Address the Gaps

*   **Acknowledge and Reframe:** Be honest, but immediately pivot to your strengths.
    *   **For CRMs:** *"I haven't had the chance to use MarketSharp or LEAP in a professional capacity yet. However, I'm a very fast learner with new software. I've already done some research on them and their role in the contractor industry. I'm confident I could be fully proficient within the first few weeks. The key for me is understanding the business goal, and the tool is just the means to get there."*
    *   **For Onboarding/Training:** *"While I haven't been responsible for a formal HR onboarding process, I have experience in mentoring and knowledge sharing. In my development projects, I've had to create clear documentation and explain complex technical concepts to others. I would apply that same clear communication style to training new team members here."*

## 3. Negotiation Strategy

Your goal is to respectfully argue for the **top of their stated range ($27.50/hr)**.

### The Pitch
Your argument is not that the market rate is higher. Your argument is that **your specific, advanced technical skills bring more value than they might expect** from a candidate for this role, and therefore you deserve the top end of *their own* range.

When the offer comes, or when they ask for your expectations, frame it like this:

> *"Thank you, I'm very excited about this opportunity. Based on our conversation and my understanding of the role, it seems to involve a great mix of administrative support, but also significant technical responsibilities like coding in WordPress, optimizing the CRM workflows, and acting as the primary technical troubleshooter. Given that I'm bringing a strong background in software development, automation, and systems logic to the table, I am seeking a rate at the top of the range you've posted, at $27.50 per hour. I'm confident that my technical problem-solving ability will allow me to deliver immediate and high-level value."*

### The Mindset
*   **You are not just an admin:** You are a developer taking on an admin role. This is a crucial distinction. You bring a different, more powerful toolkit.
*   **They are getting a bargain:** Even at the top of their range ($57k), they are getting developer-level problem-solving skills for an admin-level price. You are not being greedy by asking for the top of their range; you are simply asking them to acknowledge the extra value you bring.
*   **Be prepared to state the trade-off:** If they push back, it's okay to acknowledge the situation gracefully.
    > *"I understand. I recognize this role is a fantastic opportunity for me to gain experience in the sales and marketing operations domain, and I am very excited about that. I believe my technical skills provide a strong foundation to excel quickly, which is why I was aiming for the top of the range. I am certainly willing to consider [their counter-offer] as I am very enthusiastic about the role and the team."*

This approach shows you know your worth, frames your skills in the context of their needs, and sets a professional and collaborative tone for the negotiation.
