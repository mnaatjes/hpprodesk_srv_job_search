## 3. Job Analysis

This section provides a deeper analysis of the role's true nature, industry-standard titles, leadership level, and compensation.

### Role & Title Analysis
The title "Software Administrative Support" is very literal. Based on the responsibilities—which include CRM optimization, process improvement, data reporting, and technical support—this is a classic hybrid role common in small-to-medium-sized businesses. It blends IT support with business operations.

More common industry titles for this set of responsibilities include:
*   **Sales Operations Specialist:** Focuses on the sales process, CRM, and reporting.
*   **Marketing Operations Specialist:** Focuses on the marketing tech stack and campaign support.
*   **Business Systems Analyst:** Focuses on improving business processes through technology.
*   **Digital Operations Coordinator:** A generalist title for managing internal digital tools.

The company may be using "Administrative Support" to align with a specific internal job-leveling or pay scale. However, the duties described are more aligned with an "Operations Specialist" or "Analyst" role.

### Leadership Level
This is an **Individual Contributor** role, not a management position. 
*   **Evidence:** The role involves *supporting* team members, *assisting* with communications, and *preparing reports for* leadership. There is no mention of direct reports or team management responsibilities. 
*   **Implication:** This means you will be evaluated on your direct skills and ability to execute tasks and improve systems, not on your ability to manage people.

### Pay & Benefits Analysis
*   **Stated Pay Range:** $20.00 - $27.50 per hour.
*   **Annualized Range:** Approximately **$41,600 - $57,200** per year.
*   **Market Comparison (Portage/Kalamazoo, MI Area):** Research for a comparable title like "Sales Operations Specialist" in Michigan shows an average salary of around $90,000/year, with the low end (10th percentile) being approximately $75,000/year.
*   **Conclusion:** The provided pay scale is **significantly below the market average** for a role with these operational and technical responsibilities. This is a critical point to be aware of. The "Software Administrative Support" title may be used to justify this pay band.
*   **Benefits:** The posting only lists "Paid time off" and "Flexible schedule." The absence of any mention of health insurance, retirement plans (like a 401k), or other standard benefits is a major point of inquiry. You should be prepared to ask for details about the full benefits package during the interview.

## 6. My Key Talking Points & STAR Stories (Template)

Use the STAR method (Situation, Task, Action, Result) to structure your answers. Fill out these sections with your specific examples.

### Story 1: WordPress & Web Management
*   **Situation:** Describe a time you needed to manage or update a WordPress site.
*   **Task:** What was the specific goal? (e.g., update a page, fix a bug, add a new feature).
*   **Action:** What steps did you take? What HTML/CSS did you write?
*   **Result:** What was the outcome? How did it benefit the business or the user?

### Story 2: CRM / Process Optimization
*   **Situation:** Describe a workflow that was inefficient or manual.
*   **Task:** What was your responsibility? (e.g., improve the process, reduce errors).
*   **Action:** What specific changes did you make in the CRM or related software?
*   **Result:** What was the measurable improvement? (e.g., saved time, reduced errors, improved data accuracy).

### Story 3: Technical Troubleshooting & Support
*   **Situation:** A team member was struggling with a piece of software.
*   **Task:** Your goal was to help them resolve the issue.
*   **Action:** How did you diagnose the problem? How did you communicate the solution to them in a non-technical way?
*   **Result:** The team member was able to use the software correctly, and their frustration was relieved.

### Story 4: Onboarding & Training
*   **Situation:** A new employee joined the team and needed to learn the company's systems.
*   **Task:** You were responsible for getting them set up and trained.
*   **Action:** What was your process? Did you create documentation, do a walkthrough, etc.?
*   **Result:** The new hire was able to get up to speed quickly and felt supported.

## 7. Technical & Situational Questions (Sample Answers)

This section provides potential interview questions related to the specific tools and responsibilities, along with a strategy and a sample answer tailored to your updated background.

### Question 1: WordPress

*   **The Question:** "Describe your experience with WordPress."
*   **The Strategy:** Your experience is now very strong. Lead with your live, managed e-commerce site. Emphasize that your experience is not just with the front-end admin panel, but with the entire hosting environment, which is a major differentiator.
*   **Sample Response:**
    > "I have solid, hands-on experience across the entire WordPress lifecycle. I built and currently manage my own e-commerce site, mnaatjes.com, which runs on WooCommerce. For that project, I handled everything from the initial setup on a Bluehost server to theme customization, plugin management, and database administration via phpMyAdmin. I'm very comfortable using SSH to access the server for direct troubleshooting and have recently migrated the entire live site to a local, version-controlled Git environment. So, I can handle day-to-day content updates, but I can also manage the underlying server environment and debug code directly."

### Question 2: CRM Platforms

*   **The Question:** "This role uses MarketSharp and LEAP. Tell us about your CRM experience."
*   **The Strategy:** The core gap remains, so be honest. However, strengthen your answer by framing your workflow-mapping skills (using Figjam) as a core competency for understanding *any* business system.
*   **Sample Response:**
    > "While I haven't used MarketSharp or LEAP specifically, I'm adept at mapping out and understanding the complex business workflows that these tools manage. I frequently use platforms like Figjam to diagram user flows and business processes before I even start building a technical solution. I've already done some initial research on MarketSharp to understand its role in the contractor industry, and I'm confident I can apply my systematic approach to become proficient very quickly. For me, learning a new interface is the easy part; my strength is in understanding the underlying business logic and then finding ways to optimize it within the tool."

### Question 3: Technical Troubleshooting

*   **The Question:** "A sales representative calls you, frustrated, saying their CRM is 'broken' and they can't log in. What do you do?"
*   **The Strategy:** Your answer here becomes much stronger. You can now describe a multi-layered approach that includes server-level diagnostics, which is a skill most admins do not have.
*   **Sample Response:**
    > "My first step is to reassure the rep that I'll get it sorted out. Then I'd start with the user-facing basics: ask for the exact error message, confirm their username, and try a password reset. If that doesn't work, I'd move to the backend. I'd check the CRM's admin panel to ensure their account is active and has the correct permissions. If other users were having the issue, it would point to a system-wide problem. In that case, given my experience managing a live server, I'd be comfortable using my access to check the server's error logs directly, which often provides a much clearer picture of the root cause than the application's front-end. It's about looking at the problem from both the user's perspective and the system's perspective."

### Question 4: Project Management Tools

*   **The Question:** "How have you used tools like Asana, Monday.com, or Wekan to manage projects?"
*   **The Strategy:** Strengthen your answer by explicitly including your collaborative design and workflow-mapping tools. This shows you understand the full project lifecycle.
*   **Sample Response:**
    > "I believe in using the right tool for each stage of a project. For the initial, collaborative brainstorming and for mapping out complex workflows, I often use a freeform tool like Figjam. This helps the whole team get aligned on strategy before any work begins. Once the plan is set, I move to a more structured tool like Wekan to break the project down into actionable tasks. I create columns for each stage—like 'To Do', 'In Progress', and 'Done'—assign tasks to owners, set due dates, and use dependencies to ensure a smooth workflow. This process ensures clarity and accountability from the initial idea to the final delivery."

## 8. Salary Discussion & Negotiation Strategy

This is a sensitive topic. Your preparedness on the below-market pay is a *private strategic advantage*, not a talking point to lead with. Here is how to handle the discussion.

### The Golden Rule: Do Not Bring It Up First
In the initial interviews, your only goal is to convince them you are the best candidate. Focus entirely on the value you bring. Do not mention pay unless they do. Making them *want* you is what creates leverage for a negotiation.

### Answering: "What are your salary expectations?"
This is the most likely time the topic will come up. The goal is to anchor your value at the **top of their stated range**, justifying it with your advanced, developer-level skills.

*   **Sample Response:**
    > "Thank you for asking. I saw that the posted range for this role is between $20 and $27.50 an hour. Based on my understanding of the responsibilities—which include not just administrative tasks but also technical duties like managing the WordPress site, troubleshooting systems, and my preferred skill in PHP—I'm targeting the top end of that range, around **$27.50 an hour.** I'm confident that my technical background will allow me to contribute at a high level from day one."

*   **Why This Works:**
    *   It shows you are prepared and have read their job posting thoroughly.
    *   It's a reasonable request because it's within the budget they've already defined.
    *   It justifies your ask by connecting it to the more advanced aspects of the job, reminding them you are not a typical admin candidate.

### How to "Acknowledge the Trade-Off" (During Final Negotiation Only)
The idea of acknowledging the pay-for-experience trade-off is a tool for the final negotiation, *after* an offer is made. It is how you graciously accept an offer if they don't meet your target, without devaluing yourself.

*   **Scenario:** They offer you the job at $24/hr. You have countered with your pitch for $27.50, and they have come back saying $24/hr is their final offer.

*   **Sample Acceptance:**
    > "I understand, thank you for clarifying. While I was hoping we could get to the $27.50 mark based on the technical value I'll be bringing, I am still very excited about this opportunity. The chance to gain hands-on experience with your sales and marketing platforms in a supportive, growing company is incredibly valuable to me. For that reason, **I would be happy to accept the offer at $24 an hour.**"

*   **Why This Works:**
    *   It shows you are making a conscious, strategic business decision, not just passively accepting a low number.
    *   It frames the "valuable experience" as the reason for your flexibility.
    *   It ends the negotiation on a positive, enthusiastic, and collaborative note.
